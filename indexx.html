Total Knee Replacement

A surgical procedure to remove and replace the knee joint damaged by arthritis — restoring mobility, reducing pain, and improving quality of life.

Treatment Overview

Total Knee Replacement (TKR) is one of the most common and successful orthopaedic surgeries performed worldwide. It involves replacing the damaged or worn-out knee joint surfaces with artificial implants to relieve pain and restore function.

The surgeon removes damaged cartilage and bone from the surface of the knee joint and replaces it with a prosthesis made of metal alloys, high-grade plastics, and polymers. This is not as scary as it sounds — the implants are designed to last 15–20 years.

TKR can significantly improve quality of life, reduce chronic pain, and restore mobility in patients suffering from severe arthritis or knee injury.

Eligibility For Treatment

Any individual evaluated by a medical professional may be eligible for treatment if they meet the required criteria based on their overall medical condition and need. Typically, patients must satisfy the following conditions:

  • ✓ BMI (body mass index) is generally below 40
  • ✓ Patient is 65+ years old, or has significant bone/joint changes with no major complications
  • ✓ Willing to commit to lifestyle adjustments after surgery
  • ✓ No active infection or uncontrolled medical conditions that could increase surgical risk

Preparation Before Treatment

Preparing well before surgery ensures a smoother procedure and a much faster recovery. Your surgeon and care team will guide you through specific steps, but here's a general idea of what to expect:

  • ✓ Get a full pre-operative health assessment including blood tests and X-rays
  • ✓ Stop blood-thinning medications (like aspirin) at least a week before — check with your doctor
  • ✓ Arrange your home for easier movement post-surgery (grab bars, raised toilet seat, clear pathways)
  • ✓ Maintain a healthy weight — even small reductions can reduce surgical risk
  • ✓ Avoid eating or drinking anything 8–12 hours before surgery

About The Treatment

During a total knee replacement, the surgeon makes an incision along the front of the knee to access the joint. The damaged surfaces of the femur (thigh bone) and tibia (shin bone) are precisely removed using cutting guides, and the metal implant components are attached.

A plastic spacer is placed between the metal components to create a smooth gliding surface — mimicking the function of natural cartilage. In some cases, the underside of the kneecap (patella) is also resurfaced.

  • ✓ Performed under general or spinal anaesthesia
  • ✓ Implants are made from surgical-grade metal alloys and medical-grade polyethylene
  • ✓ Minimally invasive options available in some cases, with smaller incisions
  • ✓ Robotic-assisted surgery available at select hospitals for greater precision

Post-Treatment Care

Post-operative care is crucial for successful healing and long-term results. The first few weeks after surgery are the most important — this is where your commitment directly impacts your outcome.

  • ✓ Physiotherapy typically starts the day after surgery — early movement prevents blood clots
  • ✓ Pain medication and anti-inflammatory drugs will be prescribed — take them as directed
  • ✓ Keep the wound clean and dry; report any signs of infection (redness, swelling, discharge) immediately
  • ✓ Use a walker or crutches initially; transition to a cane as strength improves
  • ✓ Attend all follow-up appointments — X-rays will be done to monitor implant positioning

Recovery Tips

Most patients are surprised by how quickly they recover. Walking with support typically starts within 24 hours of surgery. Here are some tips to make the recovery smoother:

  • ✓ Do your physiotherapy exercises consistently — even when it feels uncomfortable
  • ✓ Use ice packs on the knee for 15–20 minutes several times a day to manage swelling
  • ✓ Elevate your leg when resting to reduce fluid build-up around the joint
  • ✓ Avoid kneeling, running, and high-impact activities for at least 6 months
  • ✓ Swimming and cycling are excellent low-impact exercises once cleared by your surgeon
  • ✓ Most patients return to driving 4–6 weeks after surgery (right leg surgery requires longer)

Frequently Asked Questions

How do I know if I need a knee replacement?
Eligibility is typically determined by your orthopaedic surgeon based on your symptoms, X-rays, and medical history. If you have severe knee pain that affects daily activities like walking or climbing stairs, and conservative treatments (physiotherapy, injections) haven't helped, it may be time to consider surgery.
Can a family member accompany me during my medical trip?
Absolutely — we highly recommend it. Having a trusted companion helps with mobility support, communication with care staff, and general emotional comfort during recovery. We can assist in arranging accommodation nearby for your companion as well.
What does the recovery timeline look like?
Most patients start walking with a walker within 24 hours of surgery. By 6 weeks, many can manage daily tasks independently. Full recovery — including return to more physical activities — typically takes 3 to 6 months. Consistent physiotherapy is the biggest factor in a smooth recovery.
How can I share my medical reports with the team?
You can share your reports via email at info@aafiyameditreats.com or upload them directly through our patient portal. Our medical coordinator will review everything and connect you with the right specialist within 24–48 hours of receiving your documents.
How long do knee implants last?
Modern knee implants are designed to last 15–20 years with proper care. Factors like body weight, activity level, and adherence to post-operative guidance all affect the longevity of the implant. Your surgeon will advise on activities to avoid to maximize the life of the implant.
